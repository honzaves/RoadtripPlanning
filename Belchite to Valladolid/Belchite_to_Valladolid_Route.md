# Belchite → Valladolid

**Backroads Across the High Plains — a driver's route guide**

A driver's line running broadly east-to-west across **Aragón, Soria and Castilla**, trading motorways for empty curves, high *meseta* plains, pine forest, and a final drop into Ribera del Duero wine country. You cross the old Christian–Muslim frontier again and again (Daroca, Calatayud, Gormaz, Peñafiel were all border fortresses), so castle-spotting is constant, and the food shifts from Aragonese *ternasco* in the east to Castilian roast meats and serious Tempranillo at the finish.

The places below are listed **in the exact order a driver encounters them**. Numbered entries (1–10) are the fixed waypoints; lettered entries (e.g. 1a, 5b) are optional detours that sit on or just off the connecting road for that leg.

## The route, stop by stop

### 1 · Belchite 🟢
*Start • 41.3047, -0.7545*

The most striking stop on the whole route and the place to begin. The **Pueblo Viejo** (Old Town) was a bustling town of ~3,800 in August 1937; by September it was a skeletal graveyard after one of the Civil War's most famous battles, in which more than 5,000 people died. Franco deliberately left it in ruins as a "living monument" and built a new town beside it, so the shattered original still stands.

The unusual angle: it's considered one of Spain's most **haunted** places, with day tours (history) and atmospheric night tours (legends and the paranormal). Both sell out — **book online in advance**; you cannot buy at the door, and the Old Town is visitable only on a guided tour from the New Town.

Film lore: Terry Gilliam shot *The Adventures of Baron Munchausen* here (crew reportedly saw two women in traditional dress who vanished when approached), and Guillermo del Toro filmed parts of *Pan's Labyrinth* among the ruins. Practicalities: sturdy shoes, no shade, bring water. Bird-lovers: the surrounding **El Planerón** steppe reserve is renowned for Dupont's Lark and sandgrouse.

### 1a · Fuendetodos 🟠
*Belchite → Daroca (A-222) • 41.3433, -0.9594*

A short hop south of the line, the **birthplace of Francisco de Goya** (born 30 March 1746). Visit his humble birth house and — more rewardingly — the **Museo del Grabado** (Engraving Museum), holding four of his great print series: the *Caprichos*, the *Disasters of War*, *Bullfighting*, and the *Follies*. A poignant pairing with Belchite, since the Civil War damaged both. Closer to the road, **Almonacid de la Cuba** has a remarkably preserved Roman dam, and you pass the eerie semi-abandoned hamlet of Rodén and salt-flat lagoons along the A-222.

### 2 · Daroca 🔵
*Belchite → Daroca (A-222) • 41.1144, -1.4144*

A walled medieval town in a ravine. Three things earn your time: **La Mina**, a 16th-century tunnel cut 600 m through the San Jorge hill (1555–1560) to divert floodwater; the **Basílica de los Sagrados Corporales**, home to the relics of a famous Eucharistic miracle (the bleeding hosts), a layer-cake from Romanesque apse to Baroque facade; and a quirky **three-storey pastry museum** opposite the Manuel Segura bakery (running since 1874, five generations).

### 2a · Anento 🟠
*Daroca → Calatayud (N-234) • 41.0698, -1.3340*

~12 km from Daroca and officially among "the prettiest villages in Spain" — a tiny medieval village with a castle, cobbled lanes, and the **Aguallueve**, a dripping-spring grotto reached by a short, pretty walk. An easy, charming leg-stretch.

### 2b · Laguna de Gallocanta 🟠
*Daroca → Calatayud (N-234) • 40.9741, -1.5063*

**Western Europe's largest saltwater lagoon** and one of its great wildlife spectacles. Autumn through spring, migrating **cranes** stop here in their thousands — up to 60,000 at peak — alongside 40+ other species; flamingos and ducks appear at other times. Interpretation centres at the lake edge have viewing windows and binoculars. Unmissable in winter; quieter but atmospheric in summer, on a high plain near 1,000 m.

### 3 · Calatayud 🔵
*Daroca → Calatayud (N-234) • 41.3532, -1.6468*

Built on the Roman city of **Bilbilis Augusta** (birthplace of the poet Martial), whose free-to-visit ruins sit ~5–6 km out. The name is from Arabic *Qal'at Ayyub*, "Job's fortress"; it's a capital of Aragonese **Mudéjar** art, with UNESCO-listed towers including the Colegiata de Santa María. Two oddities: the **leaning tower** of San Pedro de los Francos, and the **Mesón de la Dolores** (inn tied to the "La Dolores" copla legend, with a basement museum). San Juan el Real holds frescoes by a young Goya; abandoned cave houses are carved into the rock above.

### 3a · Medinaceli 🟠
*Calatayud → Arcos de Jalón • 41.1546, -2.4406*

A spectacular hilltop village and the jewel of this leg. Its showpiece is the **only three-span Roman triumphal arch in Spain**, set on a ridge near 1,200 m over the Jalón valley. Layers of Celtic, Roman, Arabic and Christian history — an Arab castle, Roman walls with an Arab gateway, the Collegiate Church, and a modern-art museum in the Ducal Palace. On the Camino del Cid.

### 4 · Arcos de Jalón 🔵
*Calatayud → Arcos de Jalón • 41.1381, -2.2630*

A frontier town under a ruined 14th-century hilltop castle, with a Gothic-Renaissance church. Arcos is modest — the treasures are minutes away as you cross into Soria: **Santa María de Huerta** (~11 km) is one of Spain's finest Cistercian monasteries, its 1215 refectory a masterpiece. Near Arcos, the **Gorges of the Jalón** (around Somaén) draw birds of prey; Roman **Arcóbriga** lies near Monreal de Ariza.

### 5 · Almazán 🔵
*Arcos de Jalón → Almazán (CL-116) • 41.5129, -2.5594*

A walled town right on the Duero, heart of the high empty plains. Three of its four medieval gates survive. Through the Villa gate is the **Plaza Mayor**, flanked by the Renaissance Palacio de los Hurtado de Mendoza and the 12th-century Romanesque **San Miguel** (unusual eight-pointed star vault). A small *postigo* passage leads to a viewpoint over the Duero.

### 5a · Berlanga de Duero 🟠
*Almazán → El Burgo de Osma (CL-116, scenic parallel route) • 41.4642, -2.8623*

A monumental town with a 15th-century castle and the late-Gothic Colegiata. Quirky detail: inside rest the remains of **Fray Tomás de Berlanga** — the friar who discovered and named the Galápagos — who brought back a **stuffed crocodile** that still hangs there. Worth swapping the fast road for the scenic Duero frontier route.

### 5b · Ermita de San Baudelio 🟠
*Almazán → El Burgo de Osma (scenic parallel route) • 41.4183, -2.7902*

Near Casillas de Berlanga: plain outside, but inside a **Mozarabic jewel** — 11th-century murals on the old Christian–Muslim frontier, around a single central pillar that fans out **like a palm tree**. One of Spain's great "secret" art sites (many frescoes were controversially removed to American museums in the 1920s; what remains is extraordinary). **Closed Mondays.**

### 5c · Rello 🟠
*Almazán → El Burgo de Osma (scenic parallel route) • 41.3739, -2.7269*

A near-perfectly preserved tiny **walled medieval village**, barely touched by time — gates, walls and old *rollo* intact. A short, atmospheric stop.

### 5d · Calatañazor 🟠
*Almazán → El Burgo de Osma (scenic parallel route) • 41.6992, -2.8175*

A medieval village **frozen in time**, with conical-chimneyed houses and a ruined castle (National Historic-Artistic Site, 1962). Famous for the legend that here, in 1002, "Almanzor lost his drum." Beside it, the **Sabinar de Calatañazor**, one of the best-preserved juniper forests on the planet (centuries-old trees over 14 m), and the **La Fuentona** karst spring.

### 6 · El Burgo de Osma 🔵
*Almazán → El Burgo de Osma (CL-116) • 41.5876, -3.0669*

A cathedral town with stone-columned arcades; the arcaded Calle Mayor and Plaza Mayor host the local evening **tapas** ritual. The Gothic-Romanesque cathedral is huge for the town's size. Nearby: **Castillo de Gormaz** (~14 km), considered the largest caliphal fortress in Europe, over the near-ghost village of Gormaz; and the rock-carved Celtiberian-Roman ruins of **Tiermes**.

### 6a · Castillo de Ucero 🟠
*El Burgo de Osma → San Leonardo de Yagüe (SO-920) • 41.7191, -3.0456*

This leg is the run into the **Cañón del Río Lobos**. Ucero has a dramatic Gothic-Templar castle on a crag and the park's interpretation centre, with the La Galiana trailhead at the source of the Ucero river — the classic walk in to the Ermita de San Bartolomé.

### 7 · San Leonardo de Yagüe 🔵
*El Burgo de Osma → San Leonardo de Yagüe (SO-920) • 41.8286, -3.0877*

The cooler, greener stretch, thick with pines. The headline is the **Cañón del Río Lobos** — limestone cliffs, a famous **griffon vulture** colony, and a dark-sky destination. Its mystical centrepiece is the **Ermita de San Bartolomé**, a 13th-century Templar chapel with an esoteric rose window; beside it a cave with Bronze Age engravings and the photogenic "Devil's Eye" arch. The walk in is short and easy (~2.5 km round trip). San Leonardo has a 16th-century bastioned castle.

### 7a · Desfiladero de la Yecla 🟠
*San Leonardo de Yagüe → Santo Domingo de Silos (BU-910) • 41.9499, -3.4423*

Crossing the pine-forested Peñas de Cervera, the natural highlight just before Silos: a **deep, narrow gorge** carved by the El Cauce stream — in places barely two metres wide — threaded by hanging bridges and walkways, with 100+ pairs of griffon vultures on the cliffs. A short, dramatic walk (~15–20 min round trip). Nearby is the 9th-century Mozarabic Ermita de Santa Cecilia.

### 8 · Santo Domingo de Silos 🔵
*San Leonardo de Yagüe → Santo Domingo de Silos (BU-910) • 41.9625, -3.4189*

The **Benedictine monastery** is the star: its 11th-century two-storey **Romanesque cloister** is rated among Europe's finest, with intricately carved capitals and a Mudéjar ceiling. In the courtyard grows the **cypress of Silos** (1883), famous from Gerardo Diego's sonnet. The crazy-but-true fact: the Silos monks hit the **British pop charts** in the 1990s with their Gregorian-chant album *Chant*. Time a visit for sung **vespers (~7 pm)**. **Closed Mondays.**

### 8a · Caleruega 🟠
*Santo Domingo de Silos → Peñafiel (VA-223) • 41.8253, -3.4858*

Descending into the Ribera del Duero, the obvious stop is the **birthplace of Santo Domingo de Guzmán** — founder of the Dominican Order, traditionally credited with originating the Rosary. Visit the Torreón de los Guzmanes, the Dominican monastery, and the region's oldest underground wine cellar. You're now in Ribera del Duero proper — wine villages like Gumiel de Izán and the Roman Bacchus mosaics at Baños de Valdearados lie nearby.

### 9 · Peñafiel 🔵
*Santo Domingo de Silos → Peñafiel (VA-223) • 41.5985, -4.1245*

A fitting finale in wine country. The castle sits on a long ridge **shaped like a great stone ship** sailing over the plateau — one of Spain's most distinctive fortresses, now the **Provincial Wine Museum** (tastings). Below it, the **Plaza del Coso**, one of Spain's first main squares — a 3,500 m² medieval square ringed by wooden-balconied houses, used for bullfights since the Middle Ages. Rock-cut wine cellars line the streets toward the river (Protos sits at the castle's foot). Literary note: home of Don Juan Manuel, author of *Tales of Count Lucanor*.

### 10 · Valladolid 🔴
*End • 41.6544, -4.7223*

Journey's end. A flat run in from Peñafiel through the heart of Tempranillo country brings you to the regional capital and the close of the crossing.

## Planning notes

- **Book ahead where required:** Belchite (guided day & night tours sell out online), Santo Domingo de Silos (closed Mondays; aim for sung vespers ~7 pm), and the Ermita de San Baudelio (closed Mondays).
- **The richest cluster** of frontier castles, hidden Mozarabic art and frozen-in-time villages lies in **central Soria, between Almazán and El Burgo de Osma** — if you have spare time anywhere, spend it there.
- **Two "famous son" pilgrimages bookend the trip:** Goya at Fuendetodos near the start, St. Dominic at Caleruega near the end.
- **GPX files:** `route_basic.gpx` (9 waypoints, ~543 km) and `route_enhanced.gpx` (waypoints + all detours, ~755 km). Both are now dense tracks snapped to real OpenStreetMap roads (originals saved in `originals_backup/`).
- **Strictly-backroads variant:** the current tracks take the fastest line, which uses ~20% *autovía* where motorways shadow the old roads. A purely motorway-free track (riding the parallel N-/regional roads throughout) can be hand-built with shaping points on request — it adds time but matches the booklet's spirit exactly.
